    Note that these modules request exclusive DAMON access on start.

> +Virtual Address Management
> +--------------------------
> +
> +The kernel reserves a large region of virtual address space for vmalloc
> +(on x86-64 this is hundreds of terabytes).  Within this region, allocated
> +and free ranges are tracked by ``struct vmap_area`` nodes organized in two
> +red-black trees — one sorted by address for the busy areas, and one

> +tree allows free-space searches in O(log n) time.

The description of the busy-area tree is inaccurate.  Since the
vmap_node sharding work the busy areas are kept in *per-node*
red-black trees (vn->busy.root inside each struct vmap_node),
not a single global tree.  Only the free-area side remains a
single global augmented rbtree (free_vmap_area_root).  The text
should reflect this N-way sharding and explain why it was done
(to reduce lock contention on vmap_area_root).

> +Guard Pages
> +-----------
> +
> +By default, each vmalloc area is surrounded by a guard page — an unmapped
> +page that causes an immediate fault if code overruns the allocation.

"Surrounded" implies pages on both sides.  The implementation
adds only one guard page (size += PAGE_SIZE in __get_vm_area_node)
at the high end of the allocation.  Adjacent allocations share
that boundary, so there is no separate lower guard page.  Could
you say "followed by a guard page" or "terminated by a guard
page" instead?

> +-----------------
> +
> +On architectures that support it, vmalloc can use PMD- or PUD-level
> +mappings instead of individual PTEs, reducing TLB pressure for large
> +allocations.

Good overview of the page allocator subsystem.  Two factual issues
below worth fixing.

> diff --git a/Documentation/mm/page_allocation.rst b/Documentation/mm/page_allocation.rst

[ ... ]

> +Per-CPU Pagesets
> +================
> +
> +Most order-0 allocations are served from per-CPU page lists (PCP) rather
> +than the global ``free_area``.  This avoids taking the zone lock on the
> +common path, which is critical for scalability on large systems.
> +
> +Each CPU maintains lists of free pages grouped by migratetype.  Pages are
> +moved between the per-CPU lists and the buddy in batches.  The batch size
> +and high watermark for each per-CPU list are tuned based on zone size and
> +the number of CPUs.
> +
> +When a per-CPU list is empty, a batch of pages is taken from the buddy.
> +When it exceeds its high watermark, excess pages are returned.
> +``lru_add_drain()`` and ``drain_all_pages()`` flush per-CPU lists when
> +the system needs an accurate count of free pages, such as during memory
> +hot-remove.

lru_add_drain() and drain_all_pages() are different per-CPU caches.
drain_all_pages() drains the PCP free-page lists (per_cpu_pages).  But
lru_add_drain() drains the LRU pagevec batches (struct cpu_fbatches in
mm/swap.c) -- pending LRU list additions that have not yet been
committed to the LRU proper.  These are unrelated to the PCP free-page
lists described in this section.  Could you either remove lru_add_drain()
from this paragraph, or add a sentence clarifying that it drains a
different per-CPU cache (pending LRU additions) rather than PCP pages?

[ ... ]

> +Memory compaction (``mm/compaction.c``) creates contiguous free blocks for
> +high-order allocations by relocating movable pages.  It runs two scanners
> +across a zone: one walks from the bottom to find movable in-use pages, the
> +other walks from the top to find free pages.  Movable pages are migrated
> +to the free locations, consolidating free space in the middle.

The description of the two scanners is correct, but the final phrase
looks wrong.  The migration scanner starts at the low PFN (bottom) and
walks upward; the free scanner starts at the high PFN (top) and walks
downward.  Movable pages found near the bottom are migrated to the free
slots found near the top.  After compaction, the region formerly
occupied by the migrated pages (the lower portion of the zone) is now
free, while the upper portion is now populated.  Free space ends up at
the bottom of the zone, not in the middle.  The scanners meet in the
middle, but that is the termination condition, not where the free space
concentrates.  Something like "leaving a large contiguous free region at
the lower end of the zone" would be more accurate.

> +With ``CONFIG_DEFERRED_STRUCT_PAGE_INIT``, only pages in the boot node's
> +lower zones are initialized during early boot — enough to get the system
> +running.  The remaining pages are initialized in parallel by worker threads
> +(via the padata framework) before they are first needed.

This description has two inaccuracies worth fixing.

First, the scope is not limited to the "boot node".  defer_init() in
mm/mm_init.c applies the same logic to every NUMA node: lower zones of
all nodes are always initialized eagerly (the comment at line 728 reads
"Always populate low zones for address-constrained allocations"), while
only the highest zone on each node is subject to deferral.

Second, deferred pages are not guaranteed to be initialized "before they
are first needed".  The kthread running deferred_init_memmap() works
through the highest zone via padata, but deferred_grow_zone() also
exists precisely to handle on-demand initialization when an allocation
arrives before the background thread has reached that page-frame range.
So pages may be initialized either in the background or on first demand,
whichever comes first.

A more accurate description: with CONFIG_DEFERRED_STRUCT_PAGE_INIT,
all zones except the highest on each node, plus an initial section of
that highest zone, are initialized synchronously during boot.  The
remaining pages in each node's highest zone are then initialized in
parallel by per-node kthreads using the padata framework, or on demand
via deferred_grow_zone() if an allocation arrives first.

Needs Discussion elf: align ET_DYN base to exec folio order for contpte mapping 🔗 WANG Rui 1p
The patch bumps the ET_DYN load alignment to exec_folio_order page size so that PIE binaries land on contpte-eligible boundaries on arm64, but it does so unconditionally for every ET_DYN binary regardless of whether the LOAD segments are actually positioned to benefit from contpte folding.
WANG Rui <r@hev.cc>

ell: align ET_DYN base to exec folio order for contpte mapping

> +			if (exec_folio_order())
> +				alignment = max(alignment,
> +					(unsigned long)PAGE_SIZE << exec_folio_order());

The ASLR entropy concern you raise is the central one here.  On arm64
with 4K base pages exec_folio_order() is 4, so this forces a minimum
64K alignment for every ET_DYN binary.  In the PT_INTERP path that
translates directly to masking 4 more bits out of arch_mmap_rnd()
for every PIE executable on the system, not just the ones that
actually benefit.

For the optimization to produce a contpte-eligible mapping, three
things must all be true simultaneously:

  1. p_vaddr of the LOAD segment is 64K-aligned (so load_bias + vaddr
     lands on a 64K boundary after the masking below).
  2. p_offset of the LOAD segment is 64K-aligned (so the file-backed
     mmap starts at a 64K-aligned page-cache offset).
  3. The segment is large enough to contain at least one full contpte
     block.

If condition 1 is not met, masking load_bias to 64K still leaves
(load_bias + vaddr) unaligned, so no contpte folding happens and the
ALSR entropy was sacrificed for nothing.  Similarly for condition 2.
A binary produced by a linker that only guarantees 4K p_align gets
the full ASLR penalty with no iTLB benefit.

There is also a side-effect on ET_DYN binaries without PT_INTERP
(static PIE, typically the dynamic linker itself).  Previously, if
maximum_alignment() returned PAGE_SIZE, the condition

    if (alignment > ELF_MIN_ALIGN)

was false and load_bias stayed 0, leaving address selection entirely
to the kernel mmap path.  With this change, alignment is bumped to
64K on arm64, which is always > ELF_MIN_ALIGN (PAGE_SIZE), so every
static PIE binary now goes through the probe-map / vm_munmap /
MAP_FIXED_NOREPLACE sequence even when its segments carry only 4K
alignment in their headers and contpte folding will not occur.  Is
that extra overhead intentional for this class of binary?

Finally, exec_folio_order() is evaluated twice in a single
statement.  Both the generic fallback and the arm64 definition are
constant, so there is no runtime cost, but storing the result in a
local makes the code easier to read and avoids any concern if a
future architecture provides a non-trivial implementation.

    unsigned int folio_order = exec_folio_order();
    if (folio_order)
        alignment = max(alignment,
                        (unsigned long)PAGE_SIZE << folio_order);

Suggested constraint, along the lines of what you describe: only
bump the alignment when at least one PT_LOAD segment has both
p_vaddr and p_offset 64K-aligned and p_filesz >= 64K.  That would
confine the ASLR cost to binaries that were actually linked with
contpte-eligible layout, while leaving everything else unchanged.

execmem_fill_trapping_insns() ensures unwritten bytes in the allocation are

PAGE_KERNEL to ROX, which is the whole point of CONFIG_ARCH_HAS_EXECMEM_ROX.

PAGE_KERNEL (writable, NX) rather than ROX.  Callers that use execmem_alloc()

not protected as the subsystem intends.

mas_store_gfp() call, mirroring what the original populate function did?

round_up(size, PMD_SIZE) (or size if the PMD-aligned allocation failed).

reclaimed by execmem_cache_clean().  That is a vmalloc memory leak whenever
alloc_size > size.

as the old __execmem_cache_lookup() did from free_areas, but the full span

The placement of `nr_pages += epfn - spfn` is wrong.  It is inside
the for_each_free_mem_range loop body but outside the inner
`while (spfn < epfn)` loop.  Because the while loop terminates only
when spfn reaches epfn, this expression always evaluates to zero.

deferred_grow_zone() is the only caller that inspects the return
value of deferred_init_memmap_chunk().  It uses it here:

  nr_pages += deferred_init_memmap_chunk(spfn, epfn, zone, false);
  ...
  pgdat->first_deferred_pfn = nr_pages ? spfn : ULONG_MAX;
  return nr_pages > 0;

With nr_pages perpetually 0, deferred_grow_zone() sets
first_deferred_pfn to ULONG_MAX on its very first invocation and
returns false, even though it just successfully initialized and freed
pages.  Any subsequent call short-circuits immediately.  This breaks
the on-demand zone-growing path that is exercised when an allocation
arrives before the background deferred-init kthreads have caught up.

The original code accumulated the count inside the inner loop:

  nr_pages += deferred_init_pages(zone, spfn, chunk_end);

Moving it out of the while loop and switching to `epfn - spfn` loses
that count entirely.  A straightforward fix is to restore the
accumulation inside the while loop:

  if (!pfn_is_kho_scratch(spfn))
    nr_pages += deferred_init_pages(zone, spfn, chunk_end);

If scratch pages should contribute to the count even though
deferred_init_pages is skipped for them, an alternative is to
save spfn before the loop and use `saved_spfn + (chunk_end - spfn)`
but the simpler form above preserves the original semantics (only
counting pages that were actually initialized).

cgroup_file_kn_lock.  Because cgroup_rm_file() cleared kn under that
same lock before calling timer_delete_sync(), no new timer_reduce()
could fire after timer_delete_sync() returned.  That made the
old guarantee sound: once timer_delete_sync() returns, no timer
callback for this cfile will ever fire again.

non-NULL (before the WRITE_ONCE clears it), then call timer_reduce()
after timer_delete_sync() has already returned.  The timer gets
re-armed.  The eventual callback in cgroup_file_notify_timer() holds
no reference to the css that owns cfile; it is just:

    cgroup_file_notify(container_of(timer, struct cgroup_file,
                                    notify_timer));

this re-armed timer fires, the callback accesses freed memory.

css_killed_work_fn() -> css_put() -> css_release() ->
queue_rcu_work(cgroup_free_wq) -> css_free_rwork_fn() ->
ss->css_free(css).  There are two queue_rcu_work() calls between
timer_delete_sync() and ss->css_free(), so css freeing takes at least
two RCU grace periods.  With CGROUP_FILE_NOTIFY_MIN_INTV = HZ/100 =

the css is freed in practice.  But there is no formal synchronization
that prevents the timer from outliving the css -- the invariant that
timer_delete_sync() was meant to enforce is simply absent now.

cfile->lock critical section, where it was protected by the lock
that cgroup_rm_file() also holds when it clears kn.  Alternatively,
cgroup_rm_file() could call timer_delete_sync() a second time after the
current one, but that is racy too for the same reason.

whether to arm the timer, keeping timer_reduce() inside the locked

folios[] array, and (b) folio_batch_add writes to folios[nr] and since
nr <= i at all times there is no aliasing.  Both hold today, but neither
is documented.  Please add a comment explaining this invariant so future
changers of folio_batch do not silently break this loop.

> +	/* One TLB flush for the batch */
> +	try_to_unmap_flush_dirty();

> +		folio = fbatch->folios[i];
> +		pageout_one(folio, ret_folios, free_folios, sc, stat, plug,

> +	} while (++i != count);
> +	folio_batch_reinit(fbatch);

All folios that passed the first-loop filter are held locked
simultaneously here, across every pageout_one() call in the second loop.

before returning, so each folio's lock window is short.  But for the
synchronous path (shmem backed by slow NFS, or a ramdisk hit that takes
longer than expected), pageout_one() can stall with all up to 31 locks
held.  Any thread wanting to fault in folio[k] will block until all
folios[0..k-1] have been paged out.  The commit message mentions this
concern briefly; it would be good to spell out what workloads are at
risk and why they are considered acceptable.
